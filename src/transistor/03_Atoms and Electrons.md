# From Matter to Atoms

Everything around us is made of **matter**.

Matter is anything that:

- Has mass
- Occupies space

Examples include:

- Water
- Air
- Wood
- Copper
- Silicon
- Plastic
- Your computer
- Your phone

For thousands of years, scientists wondered:

> "If we keep cutting a material into smaller and smaller pieces, what is the smallest piece we can get?"

The answer is the **atom**.

# What Is an Atom?

An **atom** is the smallest unit of an element that still keeps the chemical properties of that element.

Think of an atom as a tiny building block.

Just as a LEGO model is built from many LEGO bricks, everything in the universe is built from countless atoms.

Atoms are incredibly small.

- Millions of atoms could fit across the width of a pin.

Although atoms are extremely small, they are not solid balls. Most of an atom is actually **empty space**.

---

## Proton

A **proton** has a positive electric charge.

It remains inside the nucleus.

The number of protons determines which element an atom is.

Examples:

- Hydrogen вҶ’ 1 proton
- Carbon вҶ’ 6 protons
- Silicon вҶ’ 14 protons
- Copper вҶ’ 29 protons

The proton does **not** move during ordinary electrical conduction.

---

## Neutron

A **neutron** has no electric charge.

Its job is to help stabilize the nucleus.

Neutrons also stay inside the nucleus.

They are important in nuclear physics but play little direct role in ordinary electrical circuits.

---

## Electron

The **electron** is one of the most important particles in electronics.

An electron:

- Has a negative charge
- Is much lighter than a proton
- Exists outside the nucleus
- Can move from one atom to another in certain materials

It is the movement of electrons that creates electric current.

---

# Valence Electrons

Valence electrons are extremely important in computer engineering.

Why?

Because they are the electrons that can:

- Move between atoms
- Form chemical bonds
- Carry electric current

Later, when we study **conductors**, **insulators**, and **semiconductors**, the number of valence electrons will explain why different materials behave differently.

# How Electrons Move

Electrons are tightly held in some materials but loosely held in others.

If an electron can move easily from one atom to another, electricity flows more easily.

Materials differ in how easily they allow this movement.

This leads to three major categories:

- Conductors
- Insulators
- Semiconductors

We will study these in the next lesson.

# Neutral Atoms

Normally, an atom contains:

- Equal numbers of protons
- Equal numbers of electrons

Example:

```
6 Protons

6 Electrons
```

Positive and negative charges cancel each other.

The atom has **no overall charge**.

Such an atom is called **electrically neutral**.

---

# Ions

Sometimes an atom gains or loses electrons.

If it loses electrons:

```
More Protons

Fewer Electrons

Positive Ion
```

If it gains electrons:

```
More Electrons

Negative Ion
```

Ions are important in chemistry and batteries, but ordinary electrical conduction in metal wires mainly involves the movement of electrons.

# Why Silicon Is Different

Copper is an excellent conductor because its outer electrons move easily.

Silicon behaves differently.

Its outer electrons are more tightly bound.

This makes silicon a **semiconductor**, meaning its conductivity can be controlled.

That single property made modern computers possible.

Every processor, memory chip, and graphics card contains billions of silicon-based transistors.

# Common Misconceptions

### вқҢ Atoms are solid balls.

вң… Atoms contain a tiny nucleus surrounded by a large region where electrons exist.

---

### вқҢ Protons move through electrical wires.

вң… In ordinary electrical circuits, electrons move while protons remain inside atomic nuclei.

---

### вқҢ Electricity creates electrons.

вң… Electricity is caused by the movement of electrons that already exist in materials.

---

### вқҢ Every material allows electrons to move easily.

вң… Different materials hold their electrons with different strengths.

# Summary

Everything in the universe is made of atoms.

Atoms contain three main particles:

- Protons
- Neutrons
- Electrons

Among these, electrons are the most important for electronics because they can move between atoms and carry electric current.

The ease with which electrons move determines whether a material is a conductor, an insulator, or a semiconductor.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Matter is made of atoms.
- Atoms contain protons, neutrons, and electrons.
- Protons are positively charged.
- Neutrons have no charge.
- Electrons are negatively charged.
- Electrons are responsible for electric current.
- Valence electrons determine many electrical properties of materials.
- Silicon and copper behave differently because of their electron structures.

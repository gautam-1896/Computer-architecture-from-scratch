Computer architecture basics

How does a computer switch on and off?

Every computer, from 1940s giants to the phone in your pocket, is really just a huge number of on/off switches wired together. This page compares the three switches that shaped computing history: the relay, the vacuum tube, and the transistor — and lets you watch each one flip in real time.

02  Why it matters

Same job, wildly different speed

Each generation of switch did the exact same thing — turn a circuit on or off — but got roughly a million times faster than the one before it. This bar uses a logarithmic scale (each step is 1,000× faster), because a normal ruler can't fit "10 milliseconds" and "1 nanosecond" on the same page.

03  How each one works

Three ways to make a switch

Electromagnetic Relay

A coil of wire becomes an electromagnet when current flows through it. The magnet physically pulls a metal lever (the armature) onto a contact, closing a separate circuit. No electricity actually "jumps" between the two circuits — one just controls the other, like a foot pressing a pedal.

Used in: 1900s telephone exchanges, the Harvard Mark I (1944), Konrad Zuse's Z3 (1941)

Vacuum Tube

A heated metal filament (the cathode) boils electrons off its surface into a vacuum. A wire mesh (the grid) sits in the electrons' path; giving it a small voltage can block or allow the stream through to the anode — no moving parts, so it's far faster than a relay.

Used in: ENIAC (1945, ~17,000 tubes), early radios, TVs, and amplifiers

Transistor

A sliver of specially treated silicon (a semiconductor) can be made to conduct or block current depending on a small voltage at its base. There's no heating filament, no vacuum, and nothing physically moves — just electrons shifting through solid material.

Used in: Every modern CPU and phone — billions on a single chip today

05  The bigger picture

How we got from clicks to chips

1
1835–1940s

Relays run the first computers

Telegraph and telephone engineers had used relays for decades. Early computers like the Z3 and Harvard Mark I borrowed the same parts to do logic and arithmetic — reliable, but loud and slow.

2
1906–1950s

Vacuum tubes go electronic

Lee de Forest's triode (1906) let a small signal control a much larger one with no moving parts. ENIAC (1945) used about 17,000 tubes and was thousands of times faster than relay machines — but tubes ran hot and failed often.

3
1947–today

The transistor takes over

Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley invented the transistor at Bell Labs in 1947. It was smaller, cooler, and far more reliable than a tube. Decades of shrinking transistors gave us integrated circuits, then microprocessors, then the billions-of-transistor chips in today's phones and laptops.

06  New words, quickly defined

Glossary for beginners

Switch
Anything that can be fully on or fully off — the basic building block of digital logic.
Coil / electromagnet
Wire wound in loops; current through it creates a magnetic field strong enough to pull metal.
Armature
The moving metal arm inside a relay that gets pulled by the electromagnet.
Cathode
The heated part of a vacuum tube that releases electrons.
Grid
A wire mesh inside a tube that controls how many electrons get through.
Anode
The positively charged plate that collects electrons at the end of their path.
Semiconductor
A material like silicon that can be tuned to conduct electricity only under certain conditions.
Base / collector / emitter
The three connections on a transistor; a small current at the base controls a larger current between the other two.
